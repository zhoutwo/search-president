<p><b>Zachary Taylor</b> (November 24, 1784 – July 9, 1850) was the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Presidents_of_the_United_States" title="List of Presidents of the United States">12th</a> <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a>, serving from March 1849 until his death in July 1850. Before his presidency, Taylor was a career officer in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a>, rising to the rank of <a href="/wiki/Major_general_(United_States)" title="Major general (United States)">major general</a>.</p>
<p>Taylor's status as a national hero as a result of his victories in the <a href="/wiki/Mexican-American_War" title="Mexican-American War" class="mw-redirect">Mexican-American War</a> won him election to the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> despite his vague political beliefs. His top priority as president was preserving the Union, but he died seventeen months into his term, before making any progress on the status of <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_United_States" title="Slavery in the United States">slavery</a>, which had been inflaming tensions in Congress.</p>
<p>Taylor was born into a prominent family of planters who migrated westward from <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> in his youth. He was commissioned as an officer in the U.S. Army in 1808 and made a name for himself as a <a href="/wiki/Captain_(United_States)" title="Captain (United States)">captain</a> in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a>. He climbed the ranks establishing military forts along the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> and entered the <a href="/wiki/Black_Hawk_War" title="Black Hawk War">Black Hawk War</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Colonel_(United_States)" title="Colonel (United States)">colonel</a> in 1832. His success in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Seminole_War" title="Second Seminole War">Second Seminole War</a> attracted national attention and earned him the nickname "Old Rough and Ready".</p>
<p>In 1845, as the <a href="/wiki/Annexation_of_Texas" title="Annexation of Texas" class="mw-redirect">annexation of Texas</a> was underway, President <a href="/wiki/James_K._Polk" title="James K. Polk">James K. Polk</a> dispatched Taylor to the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande" title="Rio Grande">Rio Grande</a> area in anticipation of a potential battle with Mexico over the disputed Texas–Mexico border. The Mexican–American War broke out in May 1846, and Taylor led American troops to victory in a series of battles culminating in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Palo_Alto" title="Battle of Palo Alto">Battle of Palo Alto</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Monterrey" title="Battle of Monterrey">Battle of Monterrey</a>. He became a national hero, and political clubs sprang up to draw him into the upcoming <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1848" title="United States presidential election, 1848">1848 presidential election</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Whig_Party_(United_States)" title="Whig Party (United States)">Whig Party</a> convinced the reluctant Taylor to lead their ticket, despite his unclear platform and lack of interest in politics. He won the election alongside former U.S. Representative <a href="/wiki/Millard_Fillmore" title="Millard Fillmore">Millard Fillmore</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_York" title="New York">New York</a>, defeating Democratic candidates <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Cass" title="Lewis Cass">Lewis Cass</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Orlando_Butler" title="William Orlando Butler">William Orlando Butler</a>. As president, Taylor kept his distance from Congress and his cabinet, even as partisan tensions threatened to divide the Union. Debate over the slave status of the large territories claimed in the war led to threats of secession from Southerners.</p>
<p>Despite being a Southerner and a slaveholder himself, Taylor did not push for the expansion of slavery. To avoid the question, he urged settlers in <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a> and California to bypass the territorial stage and draft constitutions for <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">statehood</a>, setting the stage for the <a href="/wiki/Compromise_of_1850" title="Compromise of 1850">Compromise of 1850</a>. Taylor died suddenly of a stomach-related illness in July 1850, ensuring he would have little impact on the sectional divide that led to <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">civil war</a> a decade later.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zachary_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Taylor was born on November 24, 1784, on a <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Orange_County,_Virginia" title="Orange County, Virginia">Orange County, Virginia</a>, to a prominent family of planters of English ancestry. He is inconclusively believed to have been born at the home of his maternal grandfather, <a href="/wiki/Hare_Forest_Farm" title="Hare Forest Farm">Hare Forest Farm</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-vaNRHPnom_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vaNRHPnom-2"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> He was the third of five surviving sons in his family (a sixth died in infancy) and had three younger sisters. His mother was Sarah Dabney (Strother) Taylor. His father, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Taylor_(colonel)" title="Richard Taylor (colonel)">Richard Taylor</a>, had served as a lieutenant colonel in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Taylor was a descendant of Elder <a href="/wiki/William_Brewster_(Pilgrim)" title="William Brewster (Pilgrim)" class="mw-redirect">William Brewster</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pilgrim_(Plymouth_Colony)" title="Pilgrim (Plymouth Colony)" class="mw-redirect">Pilgrim</a> colonist leader of the <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Colony" title="Plymouth Colony">Plymouth Colony</a>, a <i><a href="/wiki/Mayflower" title="Mayflower">Mayflower</a></i> immigrant, and one of the signers of the <a href="/wiki/Mayflower_Compact" title="Mayflower Compact">Mayflower Compact</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Allerton_Jr." title="Isaac Allerton Jr." class="mw-redirect">Isaac Allerton Jr.</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Colonial_America" title="Colonial America" class="mw-redirect">colonial</a> merchant and colonel who was the son of <i>Mayflower</i> Pilgrim <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Allerton" title="Isaac Allerton">Isaac Allerton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fear_Brewster" title="Fear Brewster">Fear Brewster</a>. Taylor's second cousin through that line was <a href="/wiki/James_Madison" title="James Madison">James Madison</a>, the fourth president.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Leaving exhausted lands, his family joined the westward migration out of Virginia and settled near what developed as <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville, Kentucky</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a>. Taylor grew up in a small woodland cabin before his family moved to a brick house with increased prosperity. The rapid growth of Louisville was a boon for Taylor's father, who came to own 10,000 acres (40&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) throughout Kentucky by the start of the 19th century; he held 26 slaves to cultivate the most developed portion of his holdings. There were no formal schools on the Kentucky frontier, and Taylor had a sporadic formal education. A schoolmaster recalled Taylor as a quick learner. His early letters show a weak grasp of spelling and grammar, and his handwriting was later described as "that of a near illiterate".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_family">Marriage and family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zachary_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marriage and family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In June 1810, Taylor married <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Taylor" title="Margaret Taylor">Margaret Mackall Smith</a>, whom he had met the previous autumn in Louisville. "Peggy" Smith came from a prominent family of <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> planters; she was the daughter of Major Walter Smith, who had served in the Revolutionary War.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The couple had six children:</p>
<ul>
<li>Ann Mackall Taylor (1811–1875),<sup id="cite_ref-miller-facts_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-miller-facts-7"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> married Robert C. Wood, a U.S. Army surgeon she had met while living Fort Snelling, in 1829.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sarah_Knox_Taylor" title="Sarah Knox Taylor">Sarah Knox "Knoxie" Taylor</a> (1814–1835),<sup id="cite_ref-miller-facts_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-miller-facts-7"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> married <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_Davis" title="Jefferson Davis">Jefferson Davis</a> in 1835, whom she had met through her father at the end of the Black Hawk War; she died at 21 of <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> in <a href="/wiki/St._Francisville,_Louisiana" title="St. Francisville, Louisiana">St. Francisville, Louisiana</a>, shortly after her marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Octavia Pannell Taylor (1816–1820),<sup id="cite_ref-miller-facts_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-miller-facts-7"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> died in early childhood.</li>
<li>Margaret Smith Taylor (1819–1820),<sup id="cite_ref-miller-facts_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-miller-facts-7"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> died in infancy along with Octavia when the Taylor family was stricken with a "bilious fever."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mary_Elizabeth_Bliss" title="Mary Elizabeth Bliss">Mary Elizabeth "Betty" Taylor</a> (1824–1909),<sup id="cite_ref-miller-facts_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-miller-facts-7"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> married <a href="/wiki/William_Wallace_Smith_Bliss" title="William Wallace Smith Bliss">William Wallace Smith Bliss</a> in 1848 (he died 1853),<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> married Philip Pendleton Dandridge in 1858.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Richard_Taylor_(general)" title="Richard Taylor (general)">Richard Scott "Dick" Taylor</a> (1826–1879),<sup id="cite_ref-miller-facts_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-miller-facts-7"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Confederate_Army" title="Confederate Army" class="mw-redirect">Confederate Army</a> general,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> married Louise Marie Myrthe Bringier in 1851.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_career">Military career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zachary_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Military career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_commissions">Initial commissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zachary_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Initial commissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On May 3, 1808, Taylor joined the U.S. Army, receiving a <a href="/wiki/Commissioned_officer" title="Commissioned officer" class="mw-redirect">commission</a> as a <a href="/wiki/First_lieutenant" title="First lieutenant">first lieutenant</a> of the <a href="/wiki/7th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="7th Infantry Regiment (United States)">Seventh Infantry Regiment</a>. He was among the new officers commissioned by Congress in response to the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake%E2%80%93Leopard_Affair" title="Chesapeake–Leopard Affair">Chesapeake–Leopard Affair</a>, in which an American frigate had been boarded by the crew of a British warship, sparking calls for war.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Taylor spent much of 1809 in the dilapidated camps of New Orleans and nearby Terre aux Boeufs. He was promoted to <a href="/wiki/Captain_(United_States)" title="Captain (United States)">captain</a> in November 1810. His army duties were limited at this time, and he attended to his personal finances. Over the next several years, he began to purchase slaves and a good deal of bank stock in Louisville.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> He bought a plantation in Louisville for $95,000 that had 83 slaves attached to it, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Cypress_Grove_Plantation" title="Cypress Grove Plantation">Cypress Grove Plantation</a> near <a href="/wiki/Rodney,_Mississippi" title="Rodney, Mississippi">Rodney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_County,_Mississippi" title="Jefferson County, Mississippi">Jefferson County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, bringing the total number of slaves under him above 200.<sup id="cite_ref-ANTI-SLAVERY_REPORTER_VOL_III_1848.2C_p_194-5_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ANTI-SLAVERY_REPORTER_VOL_III_1848.2C_p_194-5-15"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> In July 1811 he was called to the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Territory" title="Indiana Territory">Indiana Territory</a>, where he assumed control of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Knox_(Vincennes)" title="Fort Knox (Vincennes)" class="mw-redirect">Fort Knox</a> after the commandant fled. In only a few weeks, he was able to restore order in the garrison, for which he was lauded by Governor <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Harrison" title="William Henry Harrison">William Henry Harrison</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="War_of_1812">War of 1812</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zachary_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: War of 1812">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/War_of_1812" title="War of 1812">War of 1812</a>, in which U.S. forces battled the British Empire and its <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Indian</a> allies, Taylor successfully defended <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fort_Harrison" title="Battle of Fort Harrison" class="mw-redirect">Fort Harrison</a> in Indiana Territory from an Indian attack commanded by the <a href="/wiki/Shawnee" title="Shawnee">Shawnee</a> <a href="/wiki/Tribal_chief" title="Tribal chief">chief</a> <a href="/wiki/Tecumseh" title="Tecumseh">Tecumseh</a>. Taylor gained recognition and received a <a href="/wiki/Brevet_(military)" title="Brevet (military)">brevet</a> (temporary) promotion to the rank of major. Later that year he joined General <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Hopkins_(congressman)" title="Samuel Hopkins (congressman)">Samuel Hopkins</a> as an aide on two expeditions: the first into the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Territory" title="Illinois Territory">Illinois Territory</a> and the second to the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tippecanoe" title="Battle of Tippecanoe">Tippecanoe</a> battle site, where they were forced to retreat in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Wild_Cat_Creek" title="Battle of Wild Cat Creek">Battle of Wild Cat Creek</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> Taylor moved his growing family to Fort Knox after the violence subsided. In spring 1814, he was called back into action under Brigadier General <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Howard_(Missouri)" title="Benjamin Howard (Missouri)">Benjamin Howard</a>. That October he supervised the construction of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Johnson" title="Fort Johnson">Fort Johnson</a>, the last toehold of the U.S. Army in the upper Mississippi River Valley. Upon Howard's death a few weeks later, Taylor was ordered to abandon the fort and retreat to <a href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" title="St. Louis, Missouri" class="mw-redirect">Saint Louis</a>. Reduced to the rank of captain when the war ended in 1815, he resigned from the army. He re-entered it a year later after gaining a commission as a major.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commanded_Fort_Howard">Commanded Fort Howard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zachary_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Commanded Fort Howard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For two years, Taylor commanded <a href="/wiki/Fort_Howard_(Wisconsin)" title="Fort Howard (Wisconsin)">Fort Howard</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Green_Bay,_Wisconsin" title="Green Bay, Wisconsin">Green Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michigan_Territory" title="Michigan Territory">Michigan Territory</a>, settlement. He then returned to Louisville and his family. In April 1819 he was promoted to the rank of lieutenant colonel and dined with President <a href="/wiki/James_Monroe" title="James Monroe">James Monroe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> In late 1821, Taylor took the 7th Infantry to <a href="/wiki/Natchitoches,_Louisiana" title="Natchitoches, Louisiana">Natchitoches, Louisiana</a>, on the Red River. On the orders of General <a href="/wiki/Edmund_P._Gaines" title="Edmund P. Gaines">Edmund P. Gaines</a>, they set out to locate a new post more convenient to the Sabine River frontier. By the following March, Taylor had established <a href="/wiki/Fort_Jesup" title="Fort Jesup">Fort Jesup</a>, at the Shield's Spring site southwest of Natchitoches. That November he was transferred to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fort_Robertson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fort Robertson (page does not exist)">Fort Robertson</a> at <a href="/wiki/Baton_Rouge" title="Baton Rouge" class="mw-redirect">Baton Rouge</a>, where he remained until February 1824.<sup id="cite_ref-Bauer_pp._40_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bauer_pp._40-21"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> He spent the next few years on recruiting duty. In late 1826 he was called to Washington, D.C., to work on an Army committee to consolidate and improve military organization. In the meantime he acquired his first Louisiana plantation and decided to move with his family to Baton Rouge as their home.<sup id="cite_ref-Bauer_pp._40_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bauer_pp._40-21"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Black_Hawk_War">Black Hawk War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zachary_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Black Hawk War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 1828, Taylor was called back to action, commanding <a href="/wiki/Fort_Snelling" title="Fort Snelling">Fort Snelling</a> in <a href="/wiki/Michigan_Territory" title="Michigan Territory">Michigan Territory</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>) on the northern Mississippi River for a year, and nearby <a href="/wiki/Fort_Crawford" title="Fort Crawford">Fort Crawford</a> for a year. After some time on furlough, when he expanded his landholdings, Taylor was promoted to colonel of the <a href="/wiki/1st_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="1st Infantry Regiment (United States)">1st Infantry Regiment</a> in April 1832.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> At that time, the <a href="/wiki/Black_Hawk_War" title="Black Hawk War">Black Hawk War</a> was beginning in the West. Taylor campaigned under General <a href="/wiki/Henry_Atkinson_(soldier)" title="Henry Atkinson (soldier)">Henry Atkinson</a> to pursue and later defend against Chief <a href="/wiki/Black_Hawk_(Sauk_leader)" title="Black Hawk (Sauk leader)">Black Hawk</a>'s forces throughout the summer. The end of the war in August 1832 signaled the end of Indian resistance to U.S. expansion in the area, and the following years were relatively quiet. During this period Taylor resisted the courtship of his 17-year-old daughter <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Knox_Taylor" title="Sarah Knox Taylor">Sarah Knox Taylor</a> and Lieutenant <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_Davis" title="Jefferson Davis">Jefferson Davis</a>, the future <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Confederate_States_of_America" title="President of the Confederate States of America">President of the Confederate States of America</a>. He respected Davis but did not approve of his daughter becoming a military wife, as he knew it was a hard life for families. Davis and Sarah Taylor married in June 1835, but she died three months later of <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> contracted on a summer visit to Davis' sister in <a href="/wiki/St._Francisville,_Louisiana" title="St. Francisville, Louisiana">St. Francisville, Louisiana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_Seminole_War">Second Seminole War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zachary_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Second Seminole War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By 1837, the <a href="/wiki/Second_Seminole_War" title="Second Seminole War">Second Seminole War</a> was underway when Taylor was directed to Florida. He defeated the <a href="/wiki/Seminole_Indians" title="Seminole Indians" class="mw-redirect">Seminole Indians</a> in the Christmas Day <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lake_Okeechobee" title="Battle of Lake Okeechobee">Battle of Lake Okeechobee</a>, which was among the largest U.S.–Indian battles of the nineteenth century. He was promoted to brigadier general in recognition of his success. In May 1838, Brig. Gen. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jesup" title="Thomas Jesup">Thomas Jesup</a> stepped down and placed Taylor in command of all American troops in Florida, a position he held for two years. His reputation as a military leader was growing, and with it, he began to be known as "Old Rough and Ready."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> 'Recommending the importation of Cuban bloodhounds to hunt down the poor Indians' Taylor was widely condemned.<sup id="cite_ref-ANTI-SLAVERY_REPORTER_VOL_III_1848.2C_p_194-5_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ANTI-SLAVERY_REPORTER_VOL_III_1848.2C_p_194-5-15"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> After his long-requested relief was granted, Taylor spent a comfortable year touring the nation with his family and meeting with military leaders. During this period, he began to be interested in politics and corresponded with President William Henry Harrison. He was made commander of the Second Department of the Army's Western Division in May 1841. The sizable territory ran from the Mississippi River westward, south of the <a href="/wiki/37th_parallel_north" title="37th parallel north">37th parallel north</a>. Stationed in <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>, Taylor enjoyed several uneventful years, spending as much time attending to his land speculation as to military matters.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mexican%E2%80%93American_War" title="Mexican–American War">Mexican–American War</a></div>
<p>In anticipation of the annexation of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Texas" title="Republic of Texas">Republic of Texas</a>, which had established independence in 1836, Taylor was sent in April 1844 to Fort Jesup in Louisiana. He was ordered to guard against any attempts by Mexico to reclaim the territory.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> He served there until July 1845, when annexation became imminent, and President <a href="/wiki/James_K._Polk" title="James K. Polk">James K. Polk</a> directed him to deploy into disputed territory in Texas, "on or near the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande" title="Rio Grande">Rio Grande</a>" near Mexico. Taylor chose a spot at <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi,_Texas" title="Corpus Christi, Texas">Corpus Christi</a>, and his <a href="/wiki/Army_of_Occupation_(Mexico)" title="Army of Occupation (Mexico)">Army of Occupation</a> encamped there until the following spring in anticipation of a Mexican attack.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Taylor's men advanced to the Rio Grande in March 1846. Polk's attempts to negotiate with Mexico had failed, and war appeared imminent. Violence broke out several weeks later, when some of Captain Seth B. Thornton's men were attacked by Mexican forces near the river.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Polk, learning of the <a href="/wiki/Thornton_Affair" title="Thornton Affair">Thornton Affair</a>, told Congress in May that a war between Mexico and the U.S. had begun.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> That same month, Taylor commanded American forces at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Palo_Alto" title="Battle of Palo Alto">Battle of Palo Alto</a> and the nearby <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Resaca_de_la_Palma" title="Battle of Resaca de la Palma">Battle of Resaca de la Palma</a>, defeating the Mexican forces, which greatly outnumbered his own.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> These victories made him a popular hero, and within weeks he received a brevet promotion to major general and a formal commendation from Congress. The national press compared him to <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Jackson" title="Andrew Jackson">Andrew Jackson</a>, both generals who had ascended to the presidency, although Taylor denied any interest in running for office. "Such an idea never entered my head," he remarked in a letter, "nor is it likely to enter the head of any sane person."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In September, Taylor inflicted heavy casualties upon the Mexican defenders at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Monterrey" title="Battle of Monterrey">Battle of Monterrey</a>. The city of <a href="/wiki/Monterrey" title="Monterrey">Monterrey</a> had been considered "impregnable", but was captured in three days, forcing Mexican forces to retreat. Taylor was criticized for signing a "liberal" truce, rather than pressing for a large-scale surrender.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Afterwards, half of Taylor's army was ordered to join General <a href="/wiki/Winfield_Scott" title="Winfield Scott">Winfield Scott</a>'s soldiers as they <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Veracruz" title="Siege of Veracruz">besieged Veracruz</a>. Mexican General <a href="/wiki/Antonio_L%C3%B3pez_de_Santa_Anna" title="Antonio López de Santa Anna">Antonio López de Santa Anna</a> discovered, through an intercepted letter from Scott, that Taylor had contributed all but 6,000 of his men to the effort. His remaining force included only a few hundred <a href="/wiki/Regular_army" title="Regular army">regular army</a> soldiers, and Santa Anna resolved to take advantage of the situation.</p>
<p>Santa Anna attacked Taylor with 20,000 men at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Buena_Vista" title="Battle of Buena Vista">Battle of Buena Vista</a> in February 1847, leaving around 700 Americans dead or wounded at a cost of over 1,500 Mexican.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>b<span>]</span></a></sup> Outmatched, the Mexican forces retreated, ensuring a "far-reaching" victory for the Americans.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In recognition of his victory at Buena Vista, Taylor was elected an honorary member of the New York <a href="/wiki/Society_of_the_Cincinnati" title="Society of the Cincinnati">Society of the Cincinnati</a> on July 4, 1847. Taylor's father had been an Original Member of the Virginia Society of the Cincinnati. Unfortunately for the younger Taylor, the Virginia society had disbanded shortly before the elder Taylor's death, which prevented him from succeeding to his father's "seat" in the Society. Taylor was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Aztec_Club_of_1847" title="Aztec Club of 1847">Aztec Club of 1847</a>, Military Society of the Mexican War.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Taylor remained at Monterrey until late November 1847, when he set sail for home. While he would spend the following year in command of the Army's entire western division, his active military career was over. In December he received a hero's welcome in New Orleans and Baton Rouge, and his popular legacy set the stage for the 1848 presidential election.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Election_of_1848">Election of 1848</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zachary_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Election of 1848">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1848" title="U.S. presidential election, 1848" class="mw-redirect">U.S. presidential election, 1848</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TaylorFillmore.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/TaylorFillmore.jpg/320px-TaylorFillmore.jpg" width="320" height="228" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/TaylorFillmore.jpg/480px-TaylorFillmore.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f5/TaylorFillmore.jpg 2x" data-file-width="617" data-file-height="439" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TaylorFillmore.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Taylor/Fillmore 1848 campaign poster</div>
</div>

<p>In his capacity as a career officer, Taylor had never reportedly revealed his political beliefs before 1848 nor voted before that time.<sup id="cite_ref-MCCAE_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCCAE-40"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> He thought of himself as an <a href="/wiki/Independent_(voter)" title="Independent (voter)">independent</a>, believing in a strong and sound banking system for the country, and thought that President Andrew Jackson should not have allowed the <a href="/wiki/Second_Bank_of_the_United_States" title="Second Bank of the United States">Second Bank of the United States</a> to collapse in 1836.<sup id="cite_ref-MCCAE_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCCAE-40"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> He believed it was impractical to talk about expanding slavery into the western areas of the U.S., as he concluded that neither cotton nor sugar (both were produced in great quantities as a result of slavery) could be easily grown there through a <a href="/wiki/Plantation_economy" title="Plantation economy">plantation economy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MCCAE_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCCAE-40"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> He was also a firm nationalist, and due to his experience of seeing many people die as a result of warfare, he believed that <a href="/wiki/Secession" title="Secession">secession</a> was not a good way to resolve national problems.<sup id="cite_ref-MCCAE_40-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCCAE-40"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Taylor, although he did not agree with their stand in favor of protective tariffs and expensive <a href="/wiki/Internal_improvements" title="Internal improvements">internal improvements</a>, aligned himself with <a href="/wiki/Whig_Party_(United_States)" title="Whig Party (United States)">Whig Party</a> governing policies: the President should not be able to veto a law, unless that law was against the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_United_States" title="Constitution of the United States" class="mw-redirect">Constitution</a>; that the office should not interfere with <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a>; and that the power of collective decision-making, as well as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Cabinet" title="United States Cabinet" class="mw-redirect">Cabinet</a>, should be strong.<sup id="cite_ref-MCCAE_40-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCCAE-40"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Well before the American victory at Buena Vista, political clubs were formed which supported Taylor for President. His support was drawn from an unusually broad assortment of political bands, including Whigs and Democrats, Northerners and Southerners, allies and opponents of national leaders such as <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay" title="Henry Clay">Henry Clay</a> and James K. Polk. By late 1846 Taylor's opposition to a presidential run began to weaken, and it became clear that his principles more closely resembled Whig orthodoxy. Still, he maintained that he would only accept election as a national, independent figure, rather than a partisan loyalist.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> Taylor declared, as the 1848 Whig Party convention approached, that he had always been a Whig in principle, but he did consider himself a <a href="/wiki/Jeffersonian_democracy" title="Jeffersonian democracy">Jeffersonian-Democrat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MCCAE_40-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCCAE-40"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Many southerners believed that Taylor supported slavery and its expansion into the new territory absorbed from Mexico, and some were angered when Taylor suggested that if he were elected President he would not veto the <a href="/wiki/Wilmot_Proviso" title="Wilmot Proviso">Wilmot Proviso</a>, which proposed against such an expansion.<sup id="cite_ref-MCCAE_40-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCCAE-40"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> This position did not enhance his support from activist antislavery elements in the Northern U.S., as these wanted Taylor to speak out strongly in support of the Proviso, not simply fail to veto it.<sup id="cite_ref-MCCAE_40-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCCAE-40"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Most <a href="/wiki/Abolitionism_in_the_United_States" title="Abolitionism in the United States">abolitionists</a> did not support Taylor, since he was a slave-owner.<sup id="cite_ref-MCCAE_40-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCCAE-40"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Many southerners also knew that Taylor supported states' rights and was opposed to protective tariffs and government spending for internal improvements.<sup id="cite_ref-MCCAE_40-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCCAE-40"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> The Whigs hoped that he put the federal union of the United States above all else.<sup id="cite_ref-MCCAE_40-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCCAE-40"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Taylor received the Whig nomination for President in 1848. For his Vice Presidential nominee the convention chose <a href="/wiki/Millard_Fillmore" title="Millard Fillmore">Millard Fillmore</a>, a prominent <a href="/wiki/New_York" title="New York">New York</a> Whig who had chaired the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_Committee_on_Ways_and_Means" title="United States House Committee on Ways and Means">House Ways and Means Committee</a> and had been a contender for Henry Clay's Vice Presidential nominee in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1844" title="United States presidential election, 1844">1844 election</a>. Fillmore's selection was largely an attempt at reconciliation with northern Whigs, who were furious at the election of a slaveowning southerner; all factions of the party were dissatisfied with the final ticket.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> Taylor continued to minimize his role in the campaign, preferring not to directly meet with voters or correspond regarding his political views. His campaign was skillfully directed by Senator <a href="/wiki/John_J._Crittenden" title="John J. Crittenden">John J. Crittenden</a> of Kentucky, a friend and early political supporter, and bolstered by a late endorsement from Senator <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Webster" title="Daniel Webster">Daniel Webster</a> of Massachusetts.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> Taylor defeated <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Cass" title="Lewis Cass">Lewis Cass</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> candidate, and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Van_Buren" title="Martin Van Buren">Martin Van Buren</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Free_Soil" title="Free Soil" class="mw-redirect">Free Soil</a> candidate.</p>
<p>Taylor ignored the Whig platform, as historian Michael F. Holt explains:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Taylor was equally indifferent to programs Whigs had long considered vital. Publicly, he was artfully ambiguous, refusing to answer questions about his views on banking, the tariff, and internal improvements. Privately, he was more forthright. The idea of a national bank "is dead, and will not be revived in my time." In the future the tariff "will be increased only for revenue"; in other words, Whig hopes of restoring the protective <a href="/wiki/Tariff_of_1842" title="Tariff of 1842">tariff of 1842</a> were vain. There would never again be surplus federal funds from public land sales to distribute to the states, and internal improvements "will go on in spite of presidential vetoes." In a few words, that is, Taylor pronounced an epitaph for the entire Whig economic program.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_and_inauguration">Transition and inauguration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zachary_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Transition and inauguration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As president-elect, Taylor kept his distance from Washington, not resigning his Western Division command until late January 1849. He spent the months following the election formulating his cabinet selections. He was deliberate and quiet about his decisions, to the frustration of his fellow Whigs. While he despised patronage and political games, he endured a flurry of advances from office-seekers looking to play a role in his administration.<sup id="cite_ref-Bauer.2C_pp._248.E2.80.93251_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bauer.2C_pp._248.E2.80.93251-45"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While he would not appoint any Democrats, Taylor wanted his cabinet to reflect the nation's diverse interests, and so apportioned the seats geographically. He also avoided choosing prominent Whigs, sidestepping such obvious selections as Henry Clay. He saw Crittenden as a cornerstone of his administration, offering him the crucial seat of Secretary of State, but Crittenden insisted on serving out the Governorship of Kentucky to which he had just been elected. Taylor settled instead on Senator John M. Clayton of Delaware, a close associate of Crittenden's.<sup id="cite_ref-Bauer.2C_pp._248.E2.80.93251_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bauer.2C_pp._248.E2.80.93251-45"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Taylor began his trek to Washington in late January, a journey rife with bad weather, delays, injuries, sickness—and an <a href="/wiki/Ashland,_Jefferson_County,_Mississippi#Deception_of_President_Taylor" title="Ashland, Jefferson County, Mississippi">abduction</a> by a family friend. Taylor finally arrived in the nation's capital on February 24 and soon met with the outgoing President Polk.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> The incumbent Democrat held a low opinion of Taylor, privately deeming him "without political information" and "wholly unqualified for the station" of President.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> Taylor spent the following week meeting with political elites, some of whom were unimpressed with his appearance and demeanor. With less than two weeks until his inauguration, he met with Clayton and hastily finalized his cabinet.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Taylor's term as president began Sunday, March 4, but his inauguration was not held until the following day out of religious concerns.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>c<span>]</span></a></sup> His inauguration speech discussed the many tasks facing the nation, but presented a governing style of deference to Congress and sectional compromise instead of assertive executive action.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> Throughout the summer of 1849, Taylor toured the northeastern U.S., to familiarize himself with a region of which he had seen little. He spent much of the trip plagued by gastrointestinal illness and returned to Washington by September.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sectional_crisis">Sectional crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zachary_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Sectional crisis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As Taylor took office, Congress faced a battery of questions related to the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Cession" title="Mexican Cession">Mexican Cession</a>, comprising three major territories acquired by the U.S. after the Mexican War: California, New Mexico, and Utah. It was unclear which of the acquisitions would achieve statehood and which would remain federal territories, while the question of their slave status threatened to bitterly divide Congress. While a southern slaveowner himself, Taylor had no particular bias toward the southern faction of Congress which sought to maintain its right to slavery. His major goal was sectional peace, preserving the Union through legislative compromise.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> As the threat of Southern secession grew, he sided increasingly with northern abolitionists such as Senator <a href="/wiki/William_H._Seward" title="William H. Seward">William H. Seward</a> of New York, even suggesting that he would sign the Wilmot Proviso to ban slavery in federal territories should such a bill reach his desk.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In Taylor's view, the best way forward was to admit California as a state rather than a federal territory, as it would leave the slavery question out of Congress's hands. The timing for statehood was in Taylor's favor, as the <a href="/wiki/Gold_Rush" title="Gold Rush" class="mw-redirect">Gold Rush</a> was well underway at the time of his inauguration, and California's population was exploding.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> The administration dispatched Rep. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Butler_King" title="Thomas Butler King">Thomas Butler King</a> to California, to test the waters and advocate on behalf of statehood, knowing that the Californians were certain to adopt an anti-slavery constitution. King found that a constitutional convention was already underway, and by October 1849, the convention unanimously agreed to join the Union—and to ban slavery within their borders.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The question of the New Mexico–Texas border was unsettled at the time of Taylor's inauguration. The territory newly won from Mexico was under federal jurisdiction, but the Texans claimed a swath of land north of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe,_New_Mexico" title="Santa Fe, New Mexico">Santa Fe</a> and were determined to include it within their borders, despite having no significant presence there. Taylor sided with the New Mexicans' claim, initially pushing to keep it as a federal territory, but eventually supported statehood so as to further reduce the slavery debate in Congress. The Texas government, under newly instated governor <a href="/wiki/P._Hansborough_Bell" title="P. Hansborough Bell" class="mw-redirect">P. Hansborough Bell</a>, tried to ramp up military action in defense of the territory against the federal government, but was unsuccessful.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Latter_Day_Saint" title="Latter Day Saint" class="mw-redirect">Latter Day Saint</a> settlers of modern-day Utah had established a provisional <a href="/wiki/State_of_Deseret" title="State of Deseret">State of Deseret</a>, an enormous swath of territory which had little hope of recognition by Congress. The Taylor administration considered combining the California and Utah territories, but instead opted to organize the <a href="/wiki/Utah_Territory" title="Utah Territory">Utah Territory</a>. To alleviate the Mormon population's concerns over religious freedom, Taylor promised they would have relative independence from Congress despite being a federal territory.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Taylor sent his only <a href="/wiki/State_of_the_Union_address" title="State of the Union address" class="mw-redirect">State of the Union report</a> to Congress in December 1849. He recapped international events and suggested several adjustments to tariff policy and executive organization, but such issues were overshadowed by the sectional crisis facing Congress. He reported on California's and New Mexico's applications for statehood, and recommended that Congress approve them as written and "should abstain from the introduction of those exciting topics of a sectional character".<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> The policy report was prosaic and unemotional, but ended with a sharp condemnation of secessionists. It had no effect on Southern legislators, who saw the admission of two free states as an existential threat, and Congress remained stalled.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_affairs">Foreign affairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zachary_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Foreign affairs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Taylor and his Secretary of State, <a href="/wiki/John_M._Clayton" title="John M. Clayton">John M. Clayton</a>, both lacked diplomatic experience, and came into office at a relatively uneventful time in American–international politics. Their shared nationalism allowed Taylor to devolve foreign policy matters to Clayton with minimal oversight, although no decisive foreign policy was established under their administration.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> As opponents of the autocratic European order, they vocally supported German and Hungarian liberals in the <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1848" title="Revolutions of 1848">revolutions of 1848</a>, although they offered little in the way of aid.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> A perceived insult from the French minister Guillaume Tell Poussin nearly led to a break in diplomatic relations until Poussin was replaced, and a <a href="/wiki/Reparation_(legal)" title="Reparation (legal)">reparation</a> dispute with Portugal resulted in harsh words from the Taylor administration. In a more positive effort, the administration arranged for two ships to assist in the United Kingdom's search for a team of British explorers, led by <a href="/wiki/John_Franklin" title="John Franklin">John Franklin</a>, who had gotten lost in the Arctic.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> While previous Whig administrations had emphasized Pacific trade as an economic imperative, the Taylor administration took no major initiative in the <a href="/wiki/Far_East" title="Far East">Far East</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Throughout 1849 and 1850, they contended with <a href="/wiki/Narciso_L%C3%B3pez" title="Narciso López">Narciso López</a>, the Venezuelan radical who led repeated <a href="/wiki/Filibuster_(military)" title="Filibuster (military)">filibustering expeditions</a> in an attempt to conquer the island of <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>. While López made generous offers to American military leaders to support him, Taylor and Clayton saw the enterprise as illegal. They issued a blockade, and later, authorized a mass arrest of López and his fellows, although the group would eventually be acquitted.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> They also confronted Spain, which had arrested several Americans on the charge of piracy, but the Spaniards eventually surrendered them to maintain good relations with the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Arguably the Taylor administration's definitive accomplishment in foreign policy was the <a href="/wiki/Clayton%E2%80%93Bulwer_Treaty" title="Clayton–Bulwer Treaty">Clayton–Bulwer Treaty</a> of 1850, regarding a proposed inter-oceanic canal through Central America. While the U.S. and Britain were on friendly terms, and the construction of such a canal was decades away from reality, the mere possibility put the two nations in an uneasy position.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> For several years, Britain had been seizing strategic points, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Mosquito_Coast" title="Mosquito Coast">Mosquito Coast</a> on the eastern coast of present-day <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a>. Negotiations were held with Britain that resulted in the landmark Clayton–Bulwer Treaty. Both nations agreed not to claim control of any canal that might be built in Nicaragua. The treaty promoted development of an Anglo-American alliance; its completion was Taylor's last action as president.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compromise_attempts_and_final_days">Compromise attempts and final days</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zachary_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Compromise attempts and final days">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Senate Majority Leader Henry Clay took a central role as Congress debated the statehood question. While his positions had some overlap with Taylor's, the president always maintained his distance from Clay. Historians disagree on his motivations for doing so.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> With assistance from Senator Daniel Webster of Massachusetts, Clay developed his landmark proposal, the <a href="/wiki/Compromise_of_1850" title="Compromise of 1850">Compromise of 1850</a>. The proposal allowed statehood for California, giving it independence on the slavery question, while the other territories would remain under federal jurisdiction. This would include the disputed parts of New Mexico, although Texas would be reimbursed for the territory. Slavery would be retained in the District of Columbia, but the slave trade would be banned. Meanwhile, a strict <a href="/wiki/Fugitive_Slave_Law" title="Fugitive Slave Law" class="mw-redirect">Fugitive Slave Law</a> would be enacted, bypassing northern legislation which had restricted Southerners from retrieving runaway slaves.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> Tensions flared as Congress negotiated and secession talks grew, culminating with a threat from Taylor to send troops into New Mexico to protect its border from Texas, with himself leading the army. Taylor also said that anyone "taken in rebellion against the Union, he would hang ... with less reluctance than he had hanged deserters and spies in Mexico." <sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> The omnibus law was a major step forward but ultimately could not pass, due to extremists on both sides.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>No great compromise reached Taylor's desk during his presidency; instead, his last days were overshadowed by the <a href="/wiki/Galphin_affair" title="Galphin affair" class="mw-redirect">Galphin affair</a>. Before joining the Taylor cabinet, Secretary of War George W. Crawford had served as a lawyer. He had been involved in a fifteen-year case, representing the descendants of a colonial trader whose services to the British crown had not been repaid at the time of the American Revolution. The British debt to George Galphin was to be assumed by the federal government, but Galphin's heirs only received payment on the debt's principal after years of litigation, and were unable to win an interest payment from the Polk administration.</p>
<p>Taylor's Treasury Secretary William M. Meredith, with the support of Attorney General Reverdy Johnson, finally signed off on the payment in April 1850. To the president's embarrassment, this payment included a legal compensation of nearly $100,000 to Crawford; two cabinet members had effectively offered a tremendous chunk of the public treasury to another. A House investigation cleared Crawford of any legal wrongdoing, but nonetheless expressed disapproval of his accepting the payment. Taylor, who had already been sketching out a re-organization of his cabinet, now had an unfolding scandal to complicate the situation.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zachary_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Zachary_Taylor-circa1850.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Zachary_Taylor-circa1850.jpg/180px-Zachary_Taylor-circa1850.jpg" width="180" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Zachary_Taylor-circa1850.jpg/270px-Zachary_Taylor-circa1850.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Zachary_Taylor-circa1850.jpg/360px-Zachary_Taylor-circa1850.jpg 2x" data-file-width="661" data-file-height="810" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Zachary_Taylor-circa1850.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Zachary Taylor in 1850</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On July 4, 1850, Taylor reportedly consumed raw fruit and iced milk after attending holiday celebrations and a fund-raising event at the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Monument" title="Washington Monument">Washington Monument</a>, which was then under construction.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> Over the course of several days, he became severely ill with an unknown digestive ailment. His doctor "diagnosed the illness as cholera morbus, a flexible mid-nineteenth-century term for intestinal ailments as diverse as diarrhea and dysentery but not related to Asiatic cholera," the latter being a widespread epidemic at the time of Taylor's death.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> The identity and source of Taylor's illness are the subject of historical speculation (see below), although it is known that several of his cabinet members had come down with a similar illness.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Fever ensued and Taylor's chance of recovery was small. On July 8, Taylor remarked to a medical attendant:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I should not be surprised if this were to terminate in my death. I did not expect to encounter what has beset me since my elevation to the Presidency. God knows I have endeavored to fulfill what I conceived to be an honest duty. But I have been mistaken. My motives have been misconstrued, and my feelings most grossly outraged.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Despite treatment, Taylor died at 10:35&#160;p.m. on July 9, 1850. He was 65 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Taylor was interred in the Public Vault of the <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Cemetery" title="Congressional Cemetery">Congressional Cemetery</a> in Washington, D.C., from July 13, 1850, to October 25, 1850. (It was built in 1835 to hold remains of notables until either the grave site could be prepared or transportation arranged to another city.) His body was transported to the Taylor Family plot where his parents were buried on the old Taylor homestead plantation known as "Springfield" in Louisville, Kentucky.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_reputation_and_memorials">Historical reputation and memorials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zachary_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Historical reputation and memorials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Zachary_Taylor_Grave.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Zachary_Taylor_Grave.JPG/220px-Zachary_Taylor_Grave.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Zachary_Taylor_Grave.JPG/330px-Zachary_Taylor_Grave.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Zachary_Taylor_Grave.JPG/440px-Zachary_Taylor_Grave.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Zachary_Taylor_Grave.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Taylor's mausoleum at the <a href="/wiki/Zachary_Taylor_National_Cemetery" title="Zachary Taylor National Cemetery">Zachary Taylor National Cemetery</a> in Louisville, Kentucky</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Because of his short tenure, Taylor is not considered to have strongly influenced the office of the Presidency or the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-MCIL_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCIL-83"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup> Some historians believe that Taylor was too inexperienced with politics, at a time when officials needed close ties with political operatives.<sup id="cite_ref-MCIL_83-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCIL-83"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup> Despite his shortcomings, the Clayton–Bulwer Treaty affecting relations with Great Britain in Central America is "recognized as an important step in scaling down the nation's commitment to <a href="/wiki/Manifest_Destiny" title="Manifest Destiny" class="mw-redirect">Manifest Destiny</a> as a policy."<sup id="cite_ref-MCIL_83-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCIL-83"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup> While <a href="/wiki/Historical_rankings_of_Presidents_of_the_United_States" title="Historical rankings of Presidents of the United States">historical rankings of Presidents of the United States</a> have generally placed Taylor in the bottom quarter of chief executives, most surveys tend to rank him as the most effective of the four Presidents from the Whig Party.</p>
<p>Taylor was the last President to <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_United_States" title="Slavery in the United States">own slaves</a> while in office. He was the third of four Whig presidents,<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84"><span>[</span>f<span>]</span></a></sup> the last being Fillmore, his successor. Taylor was also the second president to die in office, preceded by William Henry Harrison who died while serving as President nine years earlier, as well as the only President elected from <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>.</p>
<p>In 1883, the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Kentucky" title="Commonwealth of Kentucky" class="mw-redirect">Commonwealth of Kentucky</a> placed a fifty-foot monument topped by a life-sized statue of Taylor near his grave. By the 1920s, the Taylor family initiated the effort to turn the Taylor burial grounds into a national cemetery. The Commonwealth of Kentucky donated two adjacent parcels of land for the project, turning the half-acre Taylor family cemetery into 16 acres (65,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>). On May 6, 1926, the remains of Taylor and his wife (who died in 1852) were moved to the newly constructed Taylor mausoleum, made of limestone with a granite base and marble interior, nearby. The cemetery property has been designated as the <a href="/wiki/Zachary_Taylor_National_Cemetery" title="Zachary Taylor National Cemetery">Zachary Taylor National Cemetery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The US Post Office released <a href="/wiki/US_Presidents_on_US_postage_stamps" title="US Presidents on US postage stamps" class="mw-redirect">the first postage stamp issue</a> honoring Zachary Taylor on June 21, 1875, 25 years after his death. In 1938, Taylor would appear again on a US Postage stamp, this time on the 12-cent <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Issue" title="Presidential Issue">Presidential Issue of 1938</a>. Taylor's last appearance (to date, 2010) on a US postage stamp occurred in 1986 when he was honored on the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Presidents_on_U.S._postage_stamps#AMERIPEX_issues_of_1986" title="U.S. Presidents on U.S. postage stamps" class="mw-redirect">AMERIPEX presidential issue</a>. After Washington, Jefferson, Jackson and Lincoln, Zachary Taylor was the fifth American president to appear on US postage.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assassination_theories">Assassination theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zachary_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Assassination theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Almost immediately after his death, rumors began to circulate that Taylor was poisoned by pro-slavery Southerners, and similar theories persisted into the twentieth century.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1978, Hamilton Smith based his assassination theory on the timing of drugs, the lack of confirmed cholera outbreaks, and other material.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup> In the late 1980s, Clara Rising, a former professor at University of Florida, persuaded Taylor's closest living relative to agree to an exhumation so that his remains could be tested.<sup id="cite_ref-orlando_july2593_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-orlando_july2593-92"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup> The remains were exhumed and transported to the Office of the <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> Chief Medical Examiner on June&#160;17, 1991. Samples of hair, fingernail, and other tissues were removed, and radiological studies were conducted. The remains were returned to the cemetery and reinterred, with appropriate honors, in the mausoleum.<sup id="cite_ref-Marriott_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marriott-93"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Neutron_activation_analysis" title="Neutron activation analysis">Neutron activation analysis</a> conducted at <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge_National_Laboratory" title="Oak Ridge National Laboratory">Oak Ridge National Laboratory</a> revealed no evidence of poisoning, as <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> levels were too low.<sup id="cite_ref-Marriott_93-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marriott-93"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ORNL_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ORNL-94"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup> The analysis concluded Taylor had contracted "<a href="/wiki/Cholera_morbus" title="Cholera morbus" class="mw-redirect">cholera morbus</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Acute_gastroenteritis" title="Acute gastroenteritis" class="mw-redirect">acute gastroenteritis</a>", as Washington had open sewers, and his food or drink may have been contaminated. Any potential for recovery was overwhelmed by his doctors, who treated him with "<a href="/wiki/Syrup_of_ipecac" title="Syrup of ipecac">ipecac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury(I)_chloride" title="Mercury(I) chloride">calomel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Opium" title="Opium">opium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Quinine" title="Quinine">quinine</a> (at 40&#160;grains a whack), and bled and blistered him too."<sup id="cite_ref-Sampas_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sampas-95"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup> Political scientist <a href="/wiki/Michael_Parenti" title="Michael Parenti">Michael Parenti</a> questions the traditional explanation for Taylor's death. Relying on interviews and reports by forensic pathologists, he argues that the procedure used to test for arsenic poisoning was fundamentally flawed.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup> A 2010 review concludes: "there is no definitive proof that Taylor was assassinated, nor would it appear that there is definitive proof that he was not."<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
